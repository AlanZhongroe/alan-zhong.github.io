About

Short bio

I am a first-year PhD student in Astrophysics at the University of Edinburgh, working on the formation of the first stars and galaxies and the build-up of metals during cosmic dawn.

Before starting my PhD, I completed an MSc in Aeronautical Engineering at Imperial College London and a BSc in Physics with Astrophysics at the University of Manchester. My background spans cosmology, stellar evolution, fluid dynamics, and plasma / kinetic theory.

My current work focuses on computational modelling and simulation analysis of early galaxy formation. I am particularly interested in how radiation, metal enrichment, and halo assembly interact to regulate star formation in the early Universe.

Alongside the physical modelling, I enjoy developing analysis tools and simulation workflows, primarily in Python, and working with large simulation datasets on HPC systems. I also work with compiled languages such as Fortran and C++ when interacting with astrophysical simulation codes and semi-analytic models.

More broadly, I enjoy projects that connect theory, numerical modelling, and physical interpretation, whether that involves analysing cosmological simulations or exploring simplified models that clarify the underlying physics.

Current interests

  • Formation of the first stars and galaxies at cosmic dawn
  • Pop III / Pop II transition and metal enrichment
  • Formation of early galaxies and “building block” haloes
  • Radiative feedback and Lyman–Werner backgrounds in simulations
  • Computational analysis of cosmological simulations
